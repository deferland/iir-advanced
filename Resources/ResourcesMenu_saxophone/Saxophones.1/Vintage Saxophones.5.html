Vintage Saxophones

The saxophone has a rich and varied manufacturing tradition, and vintage instruments of all makes and models are available in the used marketplace. Varying makes of vintage instruments have distinct tonal characteristics - many players contend they are even more distinct from one to the other than modern instruments are - and some are more suitable for specific musical styles than others.

As a generalization, modern instruments tend to be a bit easier to play in terms of ergonomics, response, and intonation, than the saxophones from the 1930s and 1940s. I don't often recommend that my students start with a vintage instrument from that era, though the professional horns from the 1960's onwards would certainly be fine. But professional saxophonists - particularly in the jazz, blues, and pop music genres - often use vintage instruments because of the unique sound those instruments embody.

Following is a list of some of the more common vintage saxophones used today.

Selmer - Mark VI

The Mark VI is undoubtedly the most popular vintage saxophone for jazz musicians today, and used by many classical and studio musicians as well. It was produced from 1954-1980, and most revered for the core and ring to its sound.

Selmer - Mark VII

The Mark VII was Selmer's model following the Mark VI. This was a heavier instrument with a larger bore, and while not nearly as successful as the Mark VI, still represents a fine (and less expensive) professional saxophone from the 1970's. Only alto and tenor Mark VII saxophones were widely produced. Mark VIIs were more accepted by classical musicians than jazz musicians, because of their darker and slightly tubbier sounds.

Conn 6M (alto), 10M (tenor), 12M (baritone)

Produced from the 1930's onwards, these Conns are particularly used for jazz and popular music. The 10M and 12M in particular are the ones still most used today, and all of Conn's instruments from this era have a warm, full-bodied sound, gutsy tone. Conns made before the 1960s are generally more desirable.

King Super 20

Another great saxophone primarily used by jazz musicians, and made famous by greats such as Cannonball Adderly and Johnny Griffin.

Buescher -True Tone, Aristocrat, 400 (Top Hat and Cane)

The Buescher Company produced great saxophones in the early- and mid-1900's, with heavy metal, dark tones, and solid workmanship. These are three models of vintage Buescher saxophones; their horns made before the 1960s are generally more desirable.

Martin - Handcraft, Magna

Fine saxophones from the 1930s-early 1960s. The Martins are solid, heavy instruments, with traditionally warm sounds. Martins made before the mid 1960s are generally more desirable.

Buffet - Super Dynaction, S1 Prestige

Buffet is a French instrument manufacturer of fine clarinets and saxophones, among other instruments. In the saxophone world, Buffet was in many ways overshadowed by the more prominent Selmer company, but was known for high quality instruments with a brilliant tone. The S1 Prestige feature a copper body, which presents different tonal characteristics from a standard brass body.
