CLASSICAL MOUTHPIECES

Choosing a good mouthpiece is critical for saxophonists. Many players would agree that the mouthpiece and reed combination is actually the most important part of the saxophone, as it has the largest influence over our tone, intonation, articulation, and response.

The mouthpieces listed below are all good for classical playing, working in concert band and wind ensemble settings, or playing in quartets and mixed chamber ensembles. I also recommend that the beginning saxophonist choose from the list below even when working in the jazz style. As you become more advanced and your embouchure becomes more developed, then add a jazz mouthpiece to your collection.

The majority of classical saxophone mouthpieces are made of hard rubber (ebonite). Different mouthpiece manufacturers have differing measurement systems, but most refer to the tip opening, the length of the lay or facing, and the size of the chamber. The shape of the chamber (as you look through the back of the mouthpiece) also affects the tone; some are round, others square, while others are combination of those, and are shaped like a horseshoe.

And while these mouthpieces can be generally categorized in terms of darkness or brightness of sound, keep in mind that it is primarily the saxophonist that determines the tone, by way of their embouchure, voicing, airstream, reed selection, and inherent tonal concept.

As with all mouthpieces, it is important to try a few when making a purchase. Even identical mouthpieces of the same make and size can still be quite different from one another.

Selmer

Model S80 Size: C*

The Selmer S80 has long been one of the most commonly-used classical mouthpieces, with the C* being the standard facing. Professionals and students alike appreciate its ease of response, round and centered sound, and even scale.

Model: S90 Size: 180 or 190

Selmer's more recent addition to their mouthpiece line, the S90 is similar to the S80 but with perhaps a bit more brilliance and definition to the tone. My personal choice is the Selmer S90.

Vandoren

Model: Optimum Size: AL3 (alto)

Vandoren Optimum mouthpieces have round chambers, and feature ease of response and articulation with a round, warm sound. Another great option for beginners as well as advanced players.

V5 Model: A17, A27 (alto)

The V5 series has a different color spectrum than the Optimum series, with the V5 having perhaps more breadth and complexity of tone. While I don't recommend these for those just beginning, they are played with great success by top professionals and advanced students around the world.

Rousseau

Model: NC4, 4R

These mouthpieces blow easily with a smooth tone, and tend towards the darker end of the spectrum.

Caravan

A large, round chamber mouthpiece that emphasizes the lower partials in the tonal spectrum. These mouthpieces have more resistance than some of the other mouthpieces listed above.

Morgan

Model: Excalibur

Another large, round chamber mouthpiece, but more free-blowing than a Caravan.

Yamaha

Model: 4C (student)

A passable entry-level mouthpiece that is less expensive than those listed above.
